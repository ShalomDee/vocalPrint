Exhibit 01  ·  The Crisis

Three Crises.
One Blind Spot.

Every two weeks, a language vanishes. Over 40% of the world's 7,000 languages are endangered — and fewer than 15% have been digitized. But the crisis runs deeper than preservation statistics. It is a crisis of belonging, of infrastructure, and of who AI was built to hear.

01  /  The AI Listening Gap

AI Cannot Hear You.

2× the word error rate for Black speakers vs. white speakers

Major speech-to-text systems from Amazon, Apple, Google, IBM, and Microsoft all show significant racial and dialectal disparities. AI is now screening job applicants, transcribing doctor–patient conversations, and grading students — and it mishears half the population at twice the rate of the other half.

Source: ACM FAccT 2025 · Built In · Axios, Nov 2025

02  /  The Diasporic Wound

A Grief Without a Name.

"Cultural bereavement: the experience of the uprooted person resulting from loss of social structures, cultural values and self-identity."
— World Psychiatry, 2005

Second and third-generation diaspora individuals often cannot speak their heritage language fluently but deeply feel its loss. The urgency is temporal: elderly speakers are aging out now. Nobody pays for "preservation." People pay for belonging, recognition, and continuity.

03  /  The Corpus Vacuum

Infrastructure Nobody Built.

$71.77B global language services market — mostly textual

Hundreds of endangered languages exist only in oral form. When a language has no written corpus, the entire localization supply chain breaks down. Whoever builds the verified oral corpus for low-resource languages becomes the infrastructure layer for everyone downstream — AI companies, edtech, fintech, healthcare.

"The same emotional logic that drives Ancestry DNA test purchases drives VocalPrint's consumer value proposition. The product is not an archive. It is identity infrastructure."

Exhibit 03  ·  The Method

Governance Is
the Product.

The governance infrastructure is the MVP — not the app. The consent framework, data model, and community advisory structure must ship before the first recording is made.

Voice data is biometric data in several jurisdictions. Oral traditions are communally owned, not individually owned. The IP architecture for voice data has three distinct layers: contributor owns their voice, platform may own the recorded artifact, community may have claims on cultural content expressed. Getting this wrong at the start is irreversible. Getting it right is a moat.

Five Documents Before the First Recording
  • 1
    Contributor Agreement

    Plain language, not just ToS. What is collected, how it is stored, who can access it, what commercial uses are permitted or prohibited, the compensation structure, and the withdrawal mechanism.

  • 2
    Community Consent Framework

    How community-level consent is obtained, who has authority to grant it, what happens when individual and community consent conflict, and how communities can renegotiate terms over time.

  • 3
    Data Schema Specification

    Every metadata field attached to every recording: language, dialect, region, speaker age, consent permissions, community ratification status, permitted use categories. Designed by a computational linguist, not a product manager.

  • 4
    Entity Structure Document

    Certificate of incorporation as a Public Benefit Corporation — which embeds the public benefit mission legally before any investor can pressure it away. This decision must precede incorporation and fundraising.

  • 5
    Ethics Advisory Council Terms of Reference

    Composition, actual authority, decision-making process, triggers for involvement. Written before there is anything to advise on. Functional, with real veto power — not a tokenistic diversity board.

Shalom Donga

VocalPrint is not a thesis project or a hackathon entry. It is a response to a crisis I can see closing in real time — the last fluent speakers of minority and endangered languages aging out, without anything designed to preserve what they carry.

I am building this from the conviction that identity infrastructure matters more than product features, and that community governance is not a nice-to-have — it is the technical foundation. The governance framework ships before the first recording is made. That is not idealism. That is the only defensible architecture for data of this kind.

The ethical corrective must be built in from the start: communities whose voices power the commercial layer must receive ongoing, transparent benefit from it — not a one-time consent form, but structural revenue share and data sovereignty. Unlike Reddit's informal governance, VocalPrint's must be formal, documented, and legally binding, because downstream commercial use makes informal governance a liability.

"The window for preservation is closing. Every two weeks, another language vanishes. The urgency is not rhetorical — it is calendrical."

VocalPrint is currently seeking legal counsel, academic collaborators, community organizations, and mission-aligned investors to build the governance infrastructure before any product is built. The founding documents are being drafted now.

Legal Counsel

We need architects, not advisors.

IP law, data protection, indigenous cultural heritage law, and entity formation. Voice data is biometric data in several jurisdictions. Oral traditions are communally owned. The consent framework, data schema, and Public Benefit Corporation formation documents need to be built by people who understand what is at stake when communities share linguistic heritage commercially. We are looking for attorneys who specialize in data licensing, GDPR/CCPA/AI regulation, indigenous cultural property, and hybrid nonprofit–commercial structures.
